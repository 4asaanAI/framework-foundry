## 2. LinkedIn Short-Form Post (150-300 words)

### 2.1 Specifications

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| Length | 150-300 words |
| Format | Single-line paragraphs, one idea per paragraph |
| Hashtags | 3-5, placed at the end |
| Visuals | Optional but recommended (carousel, single image, or no image) |
| CTA | Soft; question or invitation to comment |
| Attribution | Individual founder or Layaa AI company page |
| Publishing frequency | 3-5x per week (combined across both founders + company page) |

### 2.2 Structure

```
[HOOK — One sentence. Standalone. Creates curiosity or states a strong position.]

[CONTEXT — 1-2 paragraphs. Set up the situation, observation, or problem.]

[INSIGHT — 1-2 paragraphs. The core point. What did you learn, build, or observe?]

[PROOF — 1 paragraph. A specific number, example, or client outcome.]

[CLOSE — 1 sentence. Reflection, question, or forward-looking statement.]

[HASHTAGS — 3-5, on a new line]
```

>

## 3. LinkedIn Long-Form Post (500-800 words)

### 3.1 Specifications

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| Length | 500-800 words |
| Format | Single-line paragraphs; use bold for emphasis sparingly |
| Hashtags | 3-5, placed at the end |
| Visuals | Strongly recommended (carousel preferred for long-form) |
| CTA | Soft to medium; link to blog, invite conversation, or offer resource |
| Attribution | Individual founder |
| Publishing frequency | 1-2x per week per founder |

### 3.2 Structure

```
[HOOK — 1-2 sentences. Strong opening that stops the scroll.]

[CONTEXT — 2-3 paragraphs. The broader landscape, a specific experience, or a trend.]

[THE ARGUMENT — 3-4 paragraphs. Your core thesis, broken into clear points.
 Each point should be:
 - A claim
 - Followed by evidence or example
 - Connected to the next point]

[CASE STUDY / PROOF — 1-2 paragraphs. A real example with specific details.]

[COUNTERPOINT — 1 paragraph. Acknowledge the other side or a limitation.]

[TAKEAWAY — 1-2 paragraphs. What the reader should think about or do differently.]

[CTA — 1 sentence. Soft invitation.]

[HASHTAGS]
```

### 3.3 Example: LinkedIn Long-Form Post

**Attribution:** Shubham Sharma

> We automated a process that was already "automated." Here's why it still saved 25 hours a month.
>
> A fintech client came to us last quarter with what they described as an automated reporting system. They had built it themselves using spreadsheet macros and a couple of Zapier workflows. On paper, it worked. In practice, someone on their team spent 6 hours every week fixing the things the "automation" broke.
>
> Here's what we're seeing more and more: the first wave of SME automation — the DIY phase — creates almost as many problems as it solves. Not because the tools are bad, but because the workflows were designed by people who were solving an immediate pain, not thinking about what happens when the business grows.
>
> **The three patterns we keep finding:**
>
> First, brittle integrations. A Zapier workflow that breaks every time a field name changes in the CRM. This is the most common issue we see. The original builder didn't account for schema changes, and now nobody on the team knows how to fix it.
>
> Second, no error handling. When a DIY automation fails at step 3 of 7, what happens? Usually nothing. The data just disappears or gets corrupted, and someone discovers the problem days later during a manual reconciliation.
>
> Third, single points of failure. One person built it, one person understands it, and when that person is on leave, the entire workflow stops.
>
> The real work is in redesigning these workflows to be resilient, not just functional. For this fintech client, we replaced their macro-and-Zapier system with a Layaa OS automation that handles the same reporting pipeline with built-in error handling, automatic retries, and alerts when something needs human attention.
>
> The result: 25 hours saved per month. But more importantly, zero "automation fires" to put out. Their team now trusts the system, which means they actually use it instead of maintaining a parallel manual process "just in case."
>
> I should be honest about the limitation here. Rebuilding an existing automation is harder to sell than building a new one. The client already thinks they've solved the problem. The conversation has to start with showing them what the current system is costing them in invisible maintenance time.
>
> If you've built automations that require babysitting, that's not a personal failure. It's a natural stage. The question is whether you're ready to move from "it technically works" to "it reliably works."
>
> Happy to compare notes if you're dealing with something similar.
>
> #Automation #AIforSMEs #LayaaAI #Fintech

## 4. Instagram Reel Script (30-60 seconds)

### 4.1 Specifications

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| Duration | 30-60 seconds |
| Format | Talking head, screen recording, or mixed |
| Captions | Required (burned in or auto-generated, verified for accuracy) |
| Music | Subtle background; never overpowers voice |
| CTA | Verbal + on-screen text in final 5 seconds |
| Posting frequency | 3-4x per week |
| Aspect ratio | 9:16 (vertical) |

### 4.2 Structure

```
[HOOK — 0-3 sec]
One sentence that creates immediate curiosity or makes a bold claim.
Appears as on-screen text AND is spoken.

[PROBLEM — 3-10 sec]
Describe the pain point the viewer relates to.
Keep it specific to Indian SMEs.

[INSIGHT — 10-30 sec]
The core content. What's the answer, approach, or lesson?
Use 2-3 key points maximum.
Visual aids (screen recording, graphics) support the narration.

[PROOF — 30-45 sec]
One specific example, stat, or outcome.

[CTA — 45-60 sec]
Tell them what to do next.
Verbal: "Follow for more" / "Link in bio" / "Comment [word] for the full breakdown"
On-screen: Layaa AI logo + CTA text
```

```

[SECTION 2: THE SOLUTION]
Heading: [How Layaa AI addresses it]
Body: 3-4 bullet points. Each starts with a verb. Each ends with a benefit.
Supporting visual: Product screenshot or workflow diagram.

[SECTION 3: PROOF]
Heading: [Results statement or "What our clients experience"]
Content: 2-3 testimonials or case study snippets with specific numbers.

[SECTION 4: HOW IT WORKS]
Heading: [Process in 3 steps]
Step 1: [Action] — [Outcome]
Step 2: [Action] — [Outcome]
Step 3: [Action] — [Outcome]

[SECTION 5: CTA]
Heading: [Invitation, not pressure]
Body: 1-2 sentences removing friction.
Primary CTA button.
Secondary CTA (softer alternative).
```

## 6. Email Campaign Sequences

### 6.1 Specifications

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| Subject line | Under 50 characters; specific, not clever |
| Preview text | Complements subject line; 40-90 characters |
| Body length | 100-200 words (promotional), up to 500 (newsletter) |
| CTA | One primary per email; repeat at end |
| Sender | Individual founder name preferred |
| Sequence length | 5 emails over 14-21 days |
| Unsubscribe | Clear and prominent; legally required |

### 6.2 Standard Nurture Sequence Structure

**Email 1: Welcome (Day 0)**
```
Subject: [Name], here's what to expect from us
Preview: Practical AI insights for Indian businesses

Body Structure:
- Thank them for [action that triggered the sequence]
- Set expectations: what you'll send, how often
- One line about Layaa AI's mission (not a pitch)
- Link to one valuable resource (guide, checklist, blog post)
- Sign-off from founder
```

**Email 2: Value (Day 3-4)**
```
Subject: The [X]-minute test for [pain point]
Preview: Try this before your next team meeting

Body Structure:
- Open with a relatable problem
- Provide a genuinely useful tip, framework, or exercise
- Make it actionable in under 10 minutes
- No product pitch
- Soft CTA: "Reply and let me know how it goes"
```

**Email 3: Case Study (Day 7-8)**
```
Subject: How [company type] saved [specific outcome]
Preview: From [X] hours of manual work to [Y]

Body Structure:
- The situation (1-2 sentences)
- The problem (1-2 sentences)
- What changed (2-3 sentences, mention Layaa AI naturally)
- The result (specific numbers)
- CTA: "Want to see if this applies to your business?"
```

**Email 4: Offer (Day 12-14)**
```
Subject: [Specific offer — e.g., "Free workflow diagnostic for your team"]
Preview: No commitment, just clarity

Body Structure:
- Reference something from a previous email
- Present the offer clearly
- Explain exactly what happens if they say yes
- Remove friction: "30-minute call, no preparation needed on your end"
- Primary CTA: Book / Schedule / Reply
```

**Email 5: Follow-up (Day 18-21)**
```
Subject: Quick question, [Name]
Preview: Just checking if the timing works

Body Structure:
- Acknowledge they're busy (1 sentence)
- Briefly restate the value (1 sentence)
- Ask a direct question: "Is this something your team is thinking about right now?"
- Provide an easy out: "If not, no problem. These emails will keep coming with useful content either way."
- CTA: Reply or book a call
```

## 7. Blog Post (1,000-2,500 words)

### 7.1 Specifications

| Attribute | Specification |
|---|---|
| Length | 1,000-2,500 words depending on depth |
| Title | Under 70 characters; includes primary keyword naturally |
| Meta description | 150-160 characters; summarizes the value |
| Structure | H1 (title), H2 (main sections), H3 (subsections) |
| Visuals | At least 1 per 500 words (diagram, screenshot, chart, or relevant image) |
| CTA | Soft CTA in conclusion; optional mid-article CTA if relevant |
| Author | Individual founder or "Layaa AI Team" |
| Publishing frequency | 2-4 posts per month |

### 7.2 Structure

```
[TITLE — H1]
[Meta description]
[Author, Date, Reading Time]

[INTRODUCTION — 2-3 sentences]
State the problem and preview the answer. Don't bury the lead.

[SECTION 1 — H2: Context / Why This Matters]
Set up the landscape. Why is this topic relevant now?
Include data or trends specific to Indian SMEs where possible.

[SECTION 2 — H2: The Core Content]
The meat of the article. 3-5 key points, each as an H3.
Each point follows: Claim → Evidence → Example → Takeaway.

[SECTION 3 — H2: Practical Application]
How the reader applies this to their business.
Step-by-step guidance, checklist, or decision framework.

[SECTION 4 — H2: Common Mistakes or Misconceptions]
2-3 things to avoid. Builds credibility through honesty.

[CONCLUSION — 2-3 sentences]
Summarize the core takeaway. End with a forward-looking statement or question.

[CTA — Soft]
Link to related resource, invite to contact, or suggest next reading.
```

## 9. Template Usage Guidelines

1. **Templates are starting points, not rigid formulas.** Adapt structure to fit the specific content need.
2. **Always check content against tone-framework.md** before publishing.
3. **Founder-attributed content must match the voice profile** in founder-voice.md.
4. **Never publish a template with placeholder text** still visible. Review every bracket.
5. **Test email subject lines and LinkedIn hooks** with at least one team member before publishing.
6. **Archive high-performing content** as new reference examples for these templates.
7. **Review and update these templates quarterly** based on performance data.
